## Video 1: The Biggest Misconception in Physics (Noether's theorem)

### Problem/exposition setup (cold open puzzle, stated before any names or theory)

Imagine you are an astronaut out drifting in deep space when you throw a rock as hard as you can. What's gonna happen to that rock? Well, you would think that it would continue with constant velocity in a straight line. That's just Newton's first law. But what actually happens is it eventually slows down and stops. So why does this happen? Where did all the rock's energy go?

At the turn of the 20th century, the problem of energy conservation baffled some of the greatest minds, including Albert Einstein. Einstein came up with a possible solution, but then a little-known unpaid mathematician named Emmy Noether proved he was wrong. And in doing so, she created a whole new paradigm for physics, one that underlies all of particle physics and explains why anything is conserved.

### Misconception dramatization (Einstein's proposed fix, and why it had to fail)

Einstein suggested that the principle of conservation of energy, long established as a bedrock of physics, might hold the key to working out the correct field equations... the best he could find was a set of equations known as the Bianchi identities. They showed that energy was conserved, but only in a completely empty universe. So for one like ours filled with stuff, they seemed useless. Hilbert was stumped.

The issue had bothered Einstein so much that he proposed a new conservation equation. It said that if we add together the energy of matter and the energy of the gravitational field, then that total remains constant... But when Noether saw this, she was convinced Einstein had made a fundamental mistake because this equation disregards the foundational principle that general relativity is built on... when she looked at Einstein's proposed energy conservation equation, it contained a pseudotensor. And as the name implies, that isn't quite a tensor. When you try to transform it from one frame of reference to another, it doesn't remain the same quantity in different frames. The gravitational energy you might observe in one frame completely disappears in another. "People were trying to stamp conservation of energy into relativity by bending the rules of mathematics."

### Analogy anchor (symmetry, made concrete before it's used as a tool)

If I align a mirror on this triangle like so, then it looks the same as without the mirror, and that's because this is an axis of symmetry... mathematicians generalize the idea of symmetry further to be any action you can take that leaves an object unchanged. So something else I could do is I could rotate this triangle by 120 degrees... If I shift this function up or down by some constant amount, call it a, then all of its y values will change. But if I differentiate that function, I get the slope, and that remains unchanged regardless of whatever constant is added... unlike the symmetries of the triangle, this is a continuous symmetry, meaning you can shift it by any amount you like.

### Structural mapping (symmetry of the universe → conservation laws, built case by case)

Imagine you are an astronaut in this universe. Since it's empty, there is nothing special about any particular point... So suppose you throw a ball; well, it'll travel at a given speed, and after a short amount of time, it will have traveled some distance. But since the laws of physics are the same here as just before, we can shift the whole universe, and we're back to the situation we started with. And we can keep doing this over and over, and this shows us that the object will continue with that same speed indefinitely. So what we've discovered is that the principle of conservation of momentum is a direct result of the fact that there's a translation symmetry in the universe.

So imagine we take a metal rod and spin it. If we let it rotate for a minute, then it will have moved through a small angle, but we can rotate the whole universe back by that same angle and now we're at the starting position again... the law of conservation of angular momentum comes from the rotational symmetry of the universe.

So what we found is that if you take the time derivative of this quantity, it's equal to zero, which means that whatever this is has to be a constant... But wait a second because this is just the total energy. So what we've discovered is that time translation symmetry is equivalent to saying that energy is conserved.

She proved that anytime you have a continuous symmetry, you get a corresponding conservation law. Translational symmetry gives you conservation of momentum, rotational symmetry gives you conservation of angular momentum, and time translation symmetry gives you conservation of energy.

### Resolution with consequence (the rock and the redshifted photon, explained — and what it costs us)

This means over large timescales, our universe is not symmetric in time. It was very different 13 billion years ago, and it'll be different billions of years from now. Since we don't have time symmetry, that also means energy, as we usually think of it, isn't conserved. "There's no reason for energy to be conserved anymore 'cause you don't have that symmetry." Think about a photon of visible light emitted 380,000 years after the Big Bang, it travels through the universe unimpeded to arrive at our telescopes, not as visible light but as a microwave. It has lost 99.9% of its energy. "Where did the energy go? Doesn't go anywhere. Energy's not conserved." And this is exactly what's happening to the rock as well. It starts off with energy, but as it travels through the expanding universe, it slows down and stops. The energy doesn't really go anywhere; it just disappears.

But if energy isn't conserved in our universe, then why does it usually seem like it is? That's because when you're looking at the short timescales that we're used to, time translation symmetry pretty much holds... So only when you look at timescales that big do you notice that energy isn't conserved.

### Deeper question (local symmetry breaks the "proper" conservation law — and produces something new)

Noether realized there are still other symmetries left. See, no matter how you're moving, the laws of physics always look the same. That's general covariance... since these transformations aren't global but local, these are called local symmetries. In a second theorem, Noether proved that for these local symmetries, you no longer get proper conservation laws like we're used to in classical physics. Instead, you get something that only works locally: a continuity equation... Now, it's as if there are little cracks appearing between different sections of pipe, between the local patches of space-time, and through those cracks, energy can leak out. When she looked at it, she realized something. It was exactly equivalent to the Bianchi identities, the half-solution Hilbert had found. He had dismissed it because it only gave you proper energy conservation in an empty universe. But now Noether proved that it was the best you could do in general relativity.

## Video 2: The Oldest Unsolved Problem in Math (Perfect Numbers)

### Problem/exposition setup

So the problem is this. Do any odd perfect numbers exist? ... take the number six for example. You can divide it by 1, 2, 3, and 6, but let's ignore 6 because that's the number itself, and now we're left with just the proper divisors. If you add them all up, you find that they add to six, which is the number itself. So numbers like this are called perfect... If only we could find a pattern that makes these numbers, then we could use that to predict more of them.

### Analogy anchor / structural mapping (Euclid's pattern, built in front of you)

Take the number one and double it, you get two now, keep doubling it... Now starting from one, add the next number to it. So 1 plus 2 equals 3. If that adds up to a prime, then you multiply it by the last number in the sequence to get a perfect number. So two times three equals six, the first perfect number... Euclid had found a way to generate even perfect numbers, but he didn't prove that this was the only way. So there could be other ways to get perfect numbers, including potentially ones that are odd.

### Productive confusion + cliffhanger (each generation inherits the unanswered half)

Descartes wrote to Mersenne, "I think I can show that there are no even perfect numbers except those of Euclid." He also believed that if an odd perfect number does exist, it must have a special form... But Descartes couldn't prove either of those statements. Instead, he wrote "As for me, I judge that one can find real odd perfect numbers. But whatever method you use, it takes a long time to look for these."

Euler proved that every even perfect number has Euclid's form. This Euclid-Euler theorem solved a 1600-year-old problem... But Euler wasn't finished yet. He also wanted to solve the problem of odd perfect numbers... even Euler couldn't prove whether they existed or not. He wrote "Whether there are any odd perfect numbers is a most difficult question."

English mathematician Peter Barlow wrote that Euler's eighth perfect number "is the greatest that ever will be discovered for as they are merely curious without being useful, it is not likely that any person will ever attempt to find one beyond it." But Barlow was wrong.

So how large would an odd perfect number have to be? "They don't exist." You don't think odd perfect numbers exist? "No, they don't exist. I wish they did. That'd be really cool if there was just this one gigantic odd, perfect number out in the universe. They don't exist." How are you convinced that they don't exist? "There is something called a heuristic argument where it's not a proof. So if we had a proof, we'd be done."

My understanding is this heuristic argument also predicts that there are no large perfect numbers even or odd. "That's true. So there's a downside... it says there shouldn't be large, even perfect numbers and we actually expect there to be infinitely many. So why do I believe the heuristic in this case and not this case? You're right. Am I being hypocritical about that?... it's not a proof." For now this is still the oldest unsolved problem in math. Euler was right when he said whether there are any odd perfect numbers is a most difficult question.

### Deeper question (why work on a problem with no guaranteed payoff)

Now, many people may think that if there are no applications to the real world, then there's no point studying it. Why should anyone care about some old unsolved problem? But I think that's the wrong approach. For more than 2000 years, number theory had no real world applications. It was just mathematicians following their curiosity... but then in the 20th century, we realized that we could take this foundation and base our cryptography on it.

"Whenever you have a group of people put their minds towards a problem, something good's gonna come out of it... as Edison said, I learned 999 ways of not making a light bulb. Eventually I got a good way to do it." Why should you do math if you don't know that it will lead anywhere? Well, because doing the math is the only way to know for sure.

## Video 3: Why It Was Almost Impossible to Make the Blue LED

### Problem/exposition setup

If only we could make blue, then we could mix red, green, and blue to make white, and every other color, unlocking LEDs for every type of lighting in the world... But blue was almost impossible to make. Throughout the 1960s, every big electronics company in the world... raced to create the blue LED. They knew it would be worth billions. Despite the efforts of thousands of researchers, nothing worked... According to a director at Monsanto, "these won't ever replace the kitchen light."

### Structural mapping (semiconductor band theory, built from the atom up)

When you have an isolated atom, each electron in that atom occupies a discrete energy level. You can think of these energy levels like individual seats from a hockey stadium... But when you bring multiple atoms together to form a solid, the outermost electrons now feel the pull, not only of their own nucleus, but of all the other nuclei as well. And as a result, their energy levels shift... instead of being identical, they become a series of closely spaced, but separate energy levels. An energy band.

In conductors, the valence band is only partially filled... In insulators, the valence band is full, and the difference in energy between the valence and conduction bands, the band gap, is large... which brings us to semiconductors. Semiconductors are similar to insulators, except the band gap is much smaller.

The size of the band gap determines the color of the light emitted. In pure silicon, the band gap is only 1.1 electron volts. So the photon released isn't visible, it's infrared light... Moving up the spectrum, you can see why the first visible light LEDs were red and then green, and why blue was so hard. A photon of blue light requires more energy, and therefore a larger band gap.

### Misconception dramatization (the entire field bet on the wrong material)

By this time, scientists had narrowed the options down to two main candidates, zinc selenide, and gallium nitride... Zinc selenide was the far more promising option... gallium nitride had been abandoned by almost everybody for three reasons. First, it was much harder to make a high quality crystal... so between the two candidates, almost all researchers were focused on zinc selenide. Nakamura surveyed the crowded field and decided that if he were going to publish five papers by himself, he'd better focus on gallium nitride, where the competition was much less fierce.

When Nakamura attended the biggest applied physics conference in Japan, the talks on zinc selenide had over 500 attendees. The talks on gallium nitride had five.

### Resolution with consequence (three breakthroughs, and the industry it created)

His trick was to add a second nozzle to the MOCVD reactor... he called his invention the two-flow reactor... Maybe all the crystal needed was energy. So he tried heating magnesium doped gallium nitride to 400 degrees Celsius in a process known as annealing. The result, a completely p-type sample... Nakamura had an advantage, his ability to customize his MOCVD reactor. This allowed him to use brute force, adjusting the reactor to pump as much indium as he could onto the gallium nitride, in the hopes that at least some would stick. To his surprise, the technique worked.

After 30 years of searching by countless scientists, Nakamura had done it. He had created a glorious, bright blue LED... "I felt like I had reached the top of Mount Fuji." In 2010, just 1% of residential lighting sales in the world were LED. In 2022, it was over half... A full switch to LEDs could save an estimated 1.4 billion tons of CO2, equivalent to taking almost half the cars in the world off the road.

### Deeper question (what the reward actually was)

I increased my salary, $60,000 after doubling... I heard you only got $170 bonus, each patent. This was all while the blue LED was generating hundreds of millions of dollars in sales... In 2001, the Japanese courts ruled with Nakamura and ordered Nichia to pay him 10 times his initial request. But Nichia appealed and the case was eventually settled with a payout of $8 million. In the end, this was only enough to cover Nakamura's legal fees. This is all he got for an invention that now comprises an $80 billion industry.

While I was learning about Nakamura's story, I realized that what set him apart from the thousands of researchers trying to unlock the blue LED, it wasn't necessarily his knowledge, but his determination, critical thinking, and problem solving skills. Where others saw dead ends, he saw potential solutions.

## Video 4: Alfred Nobel: The Man Who Fooled the World

### Problem/exposition setup (the obituary that forces the reckoning)

One morning in 1888, Alfred Nobel sat down to read the newspaper in his conservatory... "The Merchant of Death is dead. Dr. Alfred Nobel, who became rich by finding ways to kill more people faster than ever before, died yesterday." It was his own obituary. The newspaper had made a mistake... But in that moment, he saw exactly how the world would remember him, not as an inventor, but as a butcher.

### Structural mapping (why nitroglycerin is unstable, down to the molecule)

These nitro groups are what makes nitroglycerin so unstable. Oxygen is more electronegative than nitrogen. So these two oxygen atoms pull on the electrons from the nitrogen, which shifts the electron density away from this third oxygen. But now there's little shared electron density between these two atoms, which results in a much weaker bond between the nitro groups and the main chain. So you get these weak spots, and all it takes is a little bit of energy... and then those bonds snap apart... and if this happens for enough molecules at once, well, it sets off a chain reaction.

### Misconception dramatization (the obvious fix — remove the bubbles — isn't enough)

Now, you might think to make nitroglycerin safe, Alfred just has to get rid of those bubbles, right? Well, it's not that easy... even if you could remove every single bubble, you would still have a problem. That's because nitroglycerin is a lot like the liquid in this beer bottle... The impact drives the bottom of the beer bottle down faster than the liquid can follow, which creates this sudden pressure drop. And that drop vaporizes the liquid and creates bubbles. When the liquid rushes back in, those bubbles then collapse violently... a similar thing can happen in nitroglycerin. A strong impact can create new bubbles inside the liquid in a process called cavitation. And their collapse again triggers the same chain reaction.

### Resolution with consequence (kieselguhr, the bandage, and the invention that follows)

Dejected, he looked down and noticed something strange, a fine powder whispering around his feet... Alfred saw beyond their beauty to the microscopic holes in their exoskeletons... within any individual pore, there just isn't enough nitroglycerin to propagate the detonation. So now when it's bumped or shaken, the mixture won't detonate... Alfred named it dynamite.

Then one day, while working in his home lab in Paris, he cut his hand... that night, the pain woke him... his mind drifted back to the bandage, how clean it was, how fast it absorbed the blood. He bolted upright and rushed down to the lab. He thought, "If cotton is so good at absorbing blood, maybe it'll be just as good at absorbing nitroglycerin."

### Deeper question (the prize as an attempt to outrun the invention)

There's a twisted irony to the story. When Alfred Nobel created dynamite, he was trying to make explosives safer to prevent the kind of accident that killed his brother. But by making nitroglycerin easier to handle, he also made it more accessible and that made it more deadly. So why then did he create the Nobel Prizes? Was it out of the goodness of his heart? Or a desire to redeem himself in the eyes of everyone who would come after?... so maybe it doesn't matter why he did it, only that he did.
