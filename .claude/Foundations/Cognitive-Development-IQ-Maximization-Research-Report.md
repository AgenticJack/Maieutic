## Part I: IQ Fundamentals (Gf/Gc Architecture)

### Gf vs Gc

| Dimension | Fluid Intelligence (Gf) | Crystallized Intelligence (Gc) |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Abstract reasoning, pattern detection, novel problem-solving independent of prior knowledge (Raven's Matrices) | Accumulated knowledge, vocabulary, expertise, reasoning strategies |
| Peak age | Mid-20s, then gradual lifelong decline | Grows through 50s–60s; declines only in late adulthood |
| Heritability (adults) | 50–80% | 40–60% |
| Response to training | Weak far-transfer | High — grows with learning |
| Decline with aging | Begins ~25–30 | Stable through 60s |

Most IQ tests blend both. "Raising IQ" usually = accumulating Gc; raising Gf is a separate, harder question.

### Is IQ Fixed?

Gf: substantially heritable, resistant to direct training in healthy adults. Gc: highly trainable throughout life. Both true simultaneously.

- **Heritability is not immutability** — PKU analogy: fully genetic disorder causing intellectual disability, fully correctable by diet modification. Genetic cause fixed; phenotypic outcome modifiable.
- Heritability rises with age: children ~40–50% → adults ~60–80%. Cause: as autonomy increases, people select environments matching genetic tendencies, amplifying genetic differences (gene-environment correlation).
- **Turkheimer's finding:** IQ heritability ≈10% in impoverished environments vs ≈72% in affluent environments. In deprived environments, environmental limits suppress everyone below potential → interventions work best correcting deprivation, with diminishing returns in already-rich environments.

### The Height Analogy

IQ behaves like height: both ~80% heritable in adults; both have sensitive developmental windows; both show secular trends (Flynn Effect parallels historical height gains, both nutrition/healthcare-driven); both show environmental ceiling effects.

**Where it breaks down:** Height reaches a stable adult value; IQ components *diverge* — Gf declines while Gc keeps growing. Gc remains trainable for life; adult height does not. Composite IQ can rise into the 50s even as Gf hardware declines.

**Critical Developmental Windows**

| Period | Window | Highest-Leverage Interventions |
|---|---|---|
| Prenatal | Conception–birth | Iodine, folate, toxin avoidance |
| Early childhood | 0–5 yrs | Language exposure, nutrition, secure attachment |
| Middle childhood | 5–12 yrs | Formal education, literacy, numeracy |
| Adolescence | 12–25 yrs | Education continuation, sleep, substance avoidance |
| Adulthood | 25–60 yrs | Continued learning, exercise, sleep optimization |
| Late adulthood | 60+ | Cognitive reserve building, physical activity |

First 1,000 days (conception–age 2) = highest-leverage window; ROI far exceeds any later intervention.

### What Actually Moves IQ

**Large/robust gains:**
- Formal education: ~1–5 IQ points per year of schooling (causal)
- Correcting iodine deficiency: avg +13.5 IQ points in deficient populations
- Lead abatement: +1–5 IQ points per 10 μg/dL blood-lead reduction in children

**Small/moderate gains:**
- Aerobic exercise: moderate executive-function gains via BDNF + hippocampal neurogenesis
- Sleep optimization: chronic restriction to 6 hrs ≈ cognitively equivalent to 24 hrs total deprivation — reversible with adequate sleep

**Near-zero far-transfer:**
- Working memory training (n-back): improves n-back only. Melby-Lervåg et al. (2016) meta-analysis, 87 studies: near-zero Gf far-transfer.
- Commercial brain-training apps: task-specific effects only.

### The Flynn Effect

IQ rose ~3 points/decade through the 20th century across 14 nations (Flynn, 1987) — too large/fast to be genetic. Causes: nutrition, reduced lead exposure, expanded education, more abstract-reasoning-demanding environments.

**Key detail:** gains concentrated in the most abstract, Gf-loaded subtests — not vocabulary/general knowledge. Proves environmental change *can* shift population-level Gf, even if individual training effects are modest. The effect has decelerated/reversed in some high-income countries since the 1990s (cause unknown).

### Training Better Thinking (Independent of Gf)

Reasoning quality is substantially separable from raw Gf — you can become a dramatically better thinker without raising your Gf ceiling.

- **Metacognition (d = 0.45):** three components — metacognitive knowledge (understanding own cognitive processes), metacognitive monitoring (tracking comprehension in real time), metacognitive regulation (adjusting strategy based on monitoring).
- **Dual-process awareness (Kahneman):** System 1 (fast/automatic/error-prone) vs System 2 (slow/deliberate/rule-governed). Errors arise when System 1 conclusions go unscrutinized by System 2. **Actively Open-minded Thinking** (disposition to engage System 2) predicts rational reasoning over and above IQ. Debiasing strategies with empirical support: *consider the opposite* (generate reasons your judgment might be wrong); *reference class forecasting* (anchor to base rates not narratives); *pre-mortem analysis* (assume failure, work backward); *structured argumentation* (state premises/evidence/inference rules before concluding).
- **Mental models:** richness/accuracy of internal representations of system behavior determines prediction/decision quality. Build via cross-disciplinary reading, first-principles decomposition, rapid feedback loops.
- **Rationality training (Stanovich):** high IQ ≠ rational thinking — IQ predicts formal logic but weakly predicts everyday reasoning biases. Rationality is independently trainable: Bayesian updating (adjust beliefs proportional to evidence), base rate integration, scope sensitivity (distinguish problems of different magnitudes).

### Evidence-Based Recommendations by Life Stage

| Stage | Recommendations |
|---|---|
| Prenatal–5 | Maternal iodine/folate/omega-3; zero toxin exposure (lead, mercury, alcohol); maximize language-rich interaction; secure attachment, low chronic stress |
| 5–25 | Prioritize/extend formal education (strongest causal lever); 8–10 hrs sleep (adolescents); regular aerobic exercise; avoid cannabis in adolescence (dose-dependent IQ decline, Meier et al. 2012); develop metacognitive skills via structured reflection |
| 25–60 | Sleep 7–9 hrs (degradation from restriction is self-concealed); aerobic exercise 3–5x/week moderate-vigorous; deliberate knowledge accumulation in professional/intellectual domains; cross-disciplinary mental-model building; explicit debiasing practice |
| 60+ | Maintain aerobic exercise (strongest evidence-based anti-aging intervention); continue social/intellectual engagement; treat cardiovascular risk factors (primary driver of accelerated cognitive aging); leverage accumulated Gc to compensate for processing-speed decline |

### What Not to Do
Commercial brain-training apps marketed as IQ-raising; n-back training for general intelligence in healthy adults; nootropics without a specific deficiency; short-duration (<8 week) programs expecting lasting far-transfer.

### Key Takeaways
1. "True IQ" ≈ Gf — largely genetic/early-environment, peaks mid-20s, not raised by brain training in healthy adults.
2. Gc = knowledge/expertise — grows lifelong, primary adult leverage point; organized expertise amplifies cognition beyond raw Gf.
3. Height analogy works early, breaks down later — Gc keeps upgrading while Gf hardware degrades.
4. Sleep is the most underrated adult cognitive intervention — 6 hrs chronic ≈ 24 hrs total deprivation, and the decline is imperceptible to the person experiencing it.
5. Thinking quality is trainable independent of Gf — metacognition, debiasing, richer mental models.
6. Education is the most powerful IQ lever — causal +1–5 points/year via genuine immersive learning.

## Part II: Adolescent Gf/Gc Optimization

*Source: "Fluid and Crystallized Intelligence Optimization at Age 17" — applied investigation integrating with a 16-principle AI second-brain system. Builds on Part I; does not re-derive it.*

Context: learner age 17, inside the adolescent neuroplasticity window (12–25), studying AP Biology, AP Physics, AP Chemistry, Mathematics, Neuroscience, Machine Learning, Marketing. RQ1 = Gf interventions; RQ2 = Gc strategies; RQ3 = integration → new principles P17–P25; RQ4 = high-leverage practices outside system scope.

### Evidence Grading Scale
- **HIGH** — multiple RCTs/meta-analyses, adolescent samples, large effects, replicated
- **TRANSFERRED** — strong adult evidence + plausible adolescent mechanism; direct adolescent data limited
- **MODERATE** — some adolescent RCTs or strong observational data; mixed/moderate effects
- **EMERGING** — preliminary adolescent evidence, promising but unreplicated
- **PROVISIONAL** — theoretical/mechanistic plausibility, minimal direct support
- **THEORETICAL** — mechanistic speculation, no direct empirical confirmation

### Section 1: Gf Maximization at 17

#### 1.1 Adolescent Neuroplasticity Window — Biological Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description | Grade |
|---|---|---|
| Synaptic pruning | Cortex prunes weak/unused synapses + strengthens used circuits from early childhood through ~age 25 (Huttenlocher & Dabholkar, 1997). PFC (working memory, cognitive control, abstract reasoning) is among the last regions to complete pruning — active at 17. Experience-dependent: high-demand reasoning tasks may contribute to selection of stronger circuitry (Blakemore & Choudhury, 2006). | TRANSFERRED |
| Prefrontal myelination | White-matter myelination in PFC/tracts continues through mid-20s (Giedd et al., 1999; Sowell et al., 2003), increasing conduction velocity and executive-function coordination (Gf-loaded performance). Myelination-promoting interventions (aerobic exercise, sleep, nutrition) may have larger adolescent impact than adult impact. | TRANSFERRED |
| BDNF / hippocampal neurogenesis | BDNF supports synaptic plasticity, LTP, adult hippocampal neurogenesis. Adolescent brains show elevated BDNF sensitivity vs adults (Bhave et al., 1999; Dahl, 2004); adolescent hippocampus is highly responsive to exercise- and learning-induced BDNF upregulation — a leverage point for larger/more durable structural effects than in adulthood. | — |

#### 1.2 Aerobic Exercise — Strongest Modifiable Gf Lever

**Effect sizes:**
- Sibley & Etnier meta-analysis: d = 0.40 for exercise on cognitive performance (incl. adolescents)
- Hillman et al. (2008): consistent positive effects on executive function; adolescents ≥ adults in response
- Chaddock-Heyman et al. (2013): higher aerobic fitness in preadolescents/early adolescents → greater hippocampal volume, greater basal ganglia volume, superior attentional-task performance (structural, not just functional, differences)
- **Grade: HIGH for executive function/attention; MODERATE for far-transfer Gf**

**Mechanism:** Sustained aerobic activity (60–75% max HR) elevates hippocampal BDNF via lactate-mediated signaling + monoaminergic stimulation (Voss et al., 2013) → LTP → memory consolidation/learning. Operates on a still-highly-plastic adolescent hippocampus. Secondary: ↑cerebral blood flow, ↑IGF-1, dopaminergic PFC effects.

**Optimal protocol:** Aerobic > resistance for cognitive outcomes (resistance has independent exec-function effects, Chang et al., 2012).
- Modality: running/cycling/swimming/sustained aerobic, 60–75% max HR
- Duration: 20–40 min/session (dose-response flattens beyond ~45 min for acute benefit)
- Frequency: ≥3x/week; greater benefit at 4–5x/week
- Timing: 20-min aerobic bout 30–60 min before cognitive work → measurable executive-function enhancement lasting 30–60 min (Chang et al., 2012; Hillman et al., 2009)

**Contradiction/boundary condition:** Far-transfer to IQ-test performance specifically is contested — effect sizes for broad IQ (d ≈ 0.15–0.25) are smaller than for immediate post-exercise executive-function tasks. Lambourne & Tomporowski (2010): exercise *before* cognitive tasks improves outcomes; exercise *concurrent with* complex tasks impairs them. Acute transient enhancement ≠ long-term structural development.

#### 1.3 Sleep Architecture — Consolidation Window

| Component | Finding | Grade |
|---|---|---|
| Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS) | Occurs mainly first half of night; site of synaptic downscaling (Tononi & Cirelli, 2014). Synaptic homeostasis hypothesis: waking = net synaptic potentiation, SWS selectively downscales weak connections while preserving strong recently-potentiated ones — the consolidation mechanism itself. Adolescents spend proportionally more time in SWS than adults (Feinberg & Campbell, 2010), a developmental feature that makes adolescence privileged for learning-driven synaptic modification. | HIGH (consolidation); TRANSFERRED (Gf-specific) |
| REM sleep | Concentrated in second half of night; associated with cross-domain memory integration — forming associative links between non-contiguous experiences (Walker & Stickgold, 2004). Cai et al. (2009) and Wagner et al. (2004): sleep/REM facilitates insight and discovery of hidden relational patterns. Mechanistically relevant to relational reasoning (Gf-loaded tasks) and to cross-domain integration across a multi-subject curriculum. | — |
| Adolescent circadian shift | Biological phase delay of ~1.5–2 hrs vs adults from pubertal hormone changes delaying melatonin onset (Carskadon et al., 1993; Roenneberg et al., 2004). Optimal 17-y/o sleep window ≈ 11 PM–8 AM, not adult 10 PM–7 AM. Forcing adult schedule truncates the night's *latter* portion → disproportionate REM loss — biology, not preference. | — |
| Sleep deprivation & PFC | PFC is disproportionately sleep-deprivation-sensitive (Harrison & Horne, 2000). Adolescents chronically sleeping <8 hrs show degraded PFC-dependent performance and *underestimate their own impairment* (Van Dongen et al., 2003). Chronic restriction directly taxes the capacity being developed — not a study-time trade-off. | — |

#### 1.4 High-Cognitive-Demand Coursework — "Gf Exercise Hypothesis"

High-working-memory-load/abstract-reasoning tasks may, during adolescence, contribute to the circuit selection determining adult Gf (not just practice Gf).

- Deary et al. (2007) + other longitudinal studies: years of education causally predict IQ gains (+1–5 points/year, per Part I). Mechanism unspecified but plausibly cumulative high-demand-reasoning effects on PFC development. Advanced-math ↔ Gf-test correlation well-established, causality hard to isolate from selection (Ritchie & Tucker-Drob, 2018).
- **Mathematics specifically:** algebra/proof/calculus impose relational reasoning, working memory, inhibitory control closely paralleling Gf-test structure (matrix reasoning, pattern completion, relational binding) — plausibly one of the most ecologically valid Gf-training activities because it's a meaningful domain, not decontextualized brain training. **Grade: MODERATE** (correlational strong; causal Gf-vs-Gc mechanism less established).
- **Working-memory-training caveat (adolescent-specific):** Melby-Lervåg & Hulme (2013) meta-analysis: small/no far-transfer from WM training even in children/adolescents. BUT distinguish decontextualized WM training (n-back, Cogmed) from sustained academic cognitive demand — the null result for isolated WM programs may not generalize to the broader "sustained demand shapes PFC circuits" hypothesis. **Grade: EMERGING** for domain-embedded cognitive demand; **HIGH** for null result of decontextualized WM training specifically.

#### 1.5 Music Training — Promise with Caveats

- **Positive evidence:** Schellenberg (2004) RCT — music lessons produced greater IQ gains than drama lessons/no lessons over 1 year, ~+3 IQ points mean advantage. Hyde et al. (2009): 15 months instrumental training → structural brain changes in children (larger motor/auditory cortical regions). Corrigall & Trainor (2011): music training predicted cognitive ability controlling for SES and prior ability. **Grade: MODERATE** (child studies stronger than adolescent; modest effect sizes).
- **Causality problem:** Self-selection confound — families who enroll children differ systematically in education/income/parenting; continuers differ from discontinuers. Sala & Gobet (2017, 2020) meta-analyses: causal evidence for music→general cognition is weak; publication bias + methodological heterogeneity inflate apparent effects; true causal effect substantially smaller than published positive-study means.
- **Applied:** likely beneficial, but effects probably concentrated in auditory processing/fine motor/executive coordination rather than broad Gf. Not a primary Gf intervention unless already part of learner's life — then continue for compounding benefit.

#### 1.6 Second Language Acquisition — Critical Period & Transfer

- **Original claim:** Bialystok et al. (2004, 2012) — bilinguals outperform monolinguals on attention/cognitive control (esp. inhibitory control), via constant cross-language competition exercising domain-general inhibitory circuits (Gf-relevant).
- **Replication problem:** Paap et al. (2015) systematic review + Gunnerud et al. (2020) large-scale replication substantially undermine the bilingual-advantage literature — many positive results don't replicate; publication bias inflates effects. Current consensus: skeptical of robust generalizable domain-general advantage. **Grade: PROVISIONAL** (original claims not replicated at claimed magnitude).
- **What remains:** Second-language learning = high-cognitive-demand processing, substantial new-domain Gc, possible metalinguistic-awareness/attentional gains. Adolescent period (esp. before ~age 15, gradual decline into early 20s) retains elevated sensitivity for native-like phonology; grammar/lexicon acquirable lifelong. For a 17-y/o: primarily a powerful Gc-building activity with uncertain Gf-transfer.

#### 1.7 Action Video Games — Attention/Processing Speed

- Green & Bavelier (2003): action-game players outperform non-players on attentional measures; trained non-players show transfer to untrained tasks. Subsequent meta-analyses confirm attentional capacity, visual processing speed, task-switching effects, **d ≈ 0.30–0.55**. **Grade: MODERATE** for attention/processing speed.
- **Far-transfer limitation:** Boot et al. (2011) — expectation effects (players expect improvement, controls don't) inflate apparent transfer; controlling for expectation substantially diminishes far-transfer. Supports specific transfer (genuine attention/speed gains), not broad Gf. **Grade: EMERGING–PROVISIONAL** for Gf far-transfer.
- **Applied:** not prescribable as Gf intervention, but not cognitively inert if already engaged in.

#### 1.8 Mindfulness and Executive Function

- Maynard et al. (2017) meta-analysis: positive effects on attention/executive function in school-age populations, **d ≈ 0.30–0.40**. Schonert-Reichl et al. (2015) RCT: improvements in executive function, attention, emotional regulation in children. **Grade: MODERATE** (attention/exec function); **EMERGING** (Gf-specific).
- **Mechanism:** focused-attention meditation exercises same PFC attentional-control circuits as Gf-loaded tasks. Neuroimaging shows ↑PFC thickness/connectivity in regular meditators (mostly adult, long-term-practitioner studies — not the brief school interventions studied).
- **Limitation:** modest effects, methodologically heterogeneous. Not a primary Gf intervention; corroborating/synergistic role.

#### 1.9 Gf Intervention Priority Ranking (Summary Table)

| Intervention | Evidence Grade | Estimated Effect (Gf-adjacent) | Adolescent-Specific? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sleep optimization | HIGH | Large (mechanism-level) | Yes — distinct circadian biology |
| Aerobic exercise | HIGH (exec.) / MODERATE (Gf) | d = 0.30–0.55 | Yes — elevated BDNF sensitivity |
| High-demand coursework | MODERATE | Meaningful, poorly isolated | Yes — coincides with pruning |
| Music training | MODERATE | d = 0.20–0.40 (contested) | Stronger in younger adolescents |
| Mindfulness | MODERATE | d = 0.30–0.40 (exec. function) | Not strongly age-specific |
| Action video games | MODERATE (attention) / PROVISIONAL (Gf) | d = 0.30–0.55 (attention only) | Not strongly age-specific |
| Second language learning | PROVISIONAL (Gf) | Small/unclear | Partial (phonology only) |
| Decontextualized WM training | HIGH (null result) | Near-zero far-transfer | Null result likely applies |

### Section 2: Gc Maximization at 17

#### 2.1 Structure of Gc

Gc = organized architecture of schemas, procedural knowledge, semantic networks, reasoning heuristics — not raw fact accumulation. Expert-novice difference is **structural**, not quantitative: expert knowledge is chunked/schematized for pattern recognition, transfer, inference. Gc-maximization evidence = expertise/schema-development evidence.

#### 2.2 Schema Building — Foundational Mechanism

- **Schemas** (Bartlett, 1932; formalized Rumelhart, 1980): organized knowledge structures representing object/event/situation classes with default values + relational structure. Chi et al. (1981): expert physicists organize problems by deep structural features (conservation principles) vs novices' surface features (inclined planes, pulleys) — this organization underlies superior problem-solving. **Grade: HIGH** for schema theory and expert-novice distinction.
- **Acquisition:** schemas are constructed, not transmitted by reading/lecture — learner must actively relate new info to existing schemas, identify cross-instance structural patterns, revise schema boundaries via disconfirming cases.
- **Ausubel's Assimilation Theory / Advance Organizers** (1960, 1968): meaningful learning anchors new material to existing cognitive structure; advance organizers (abstract conceptual frameworks presented before new material) activate relevant prior knowledge and provide a structural frame. Luiten et al. (1980) meta-analysis: **d ≈ 0.37** (positive but modest). **Grade: MODERATE** (advance-organizer effect); **HIGH** (prior-knowledge-activation mechanism). → For a multi-subject learner: explicitly activate prior schema before new material as a primary strategy.

#### 2.3 Elaborative Interrogation and Self-Explanation

- **Elaborative interrogation:** generating "why is this true?" explanations from existing knowledge for stated facts. Willingham (2004), Pressley et al. (1987): significantly improves memory for factual material vs reading/rereading — "why" forces connection to existing schemas, creating more encoding pathways. Meta-analysis: **d ≈ 0.60** vs reading controls. **Grade: HIGH** (declarative Gc material).
- **Self-explanation:** Chi et al. (1989, 1994) — spontaneous self-explanation of worked examples (filling inferential gaps, connecting steps to principles, noting confusion) distinguishes high- from low-performers in physics/biology; robust across domains/ages. **Grade: HIGH**.
- **System link:** P11 (Prepare→Attempt→Consolidate) structurally instantiates elaborative interrogation in Attempt phase but may not explicitly require "why" generation before consulting materials — a specific protocol could strengthen this.

#### 2.4 Interleaving vs Blocked Practice

**Counterintuitive finding:** Blocked practice (all problems of one type, then next type) *feels* more productive and produces better immediate test performance. Interleaved practice (mixed types within a session) feels harder, worse on immediate performance, but produces dramatically better **long-term retention and transfer**. Kornell & Bjork (2008) — category learning; Rohrer et al. (2014) — replicated in 7th-grade math, ecologically valid classroom study. **Grade: HIGH** (declarative and procedural Gc material).

**Mechanisms (both likely contribute):**
1. *Discrimination hypothesis* — interleaving forces identifying which strategy/concept applies to each problem, building pattern-recognition for transfer. Particularly relevant to Gc: selecting the relevant schema per problem (vs repeating one schema) builds expert-like discriminative knowledge.
2. *Spacing hypothesis* — interleaving introduces temporal spacing between repetitions of each category, activating spacing effects.

**Application:** For a 7-subject learner (AP Bio/Physics/Chem, Math, Neuroscience, ML, Marketing), interleaving across subjects within a session is particularly beneficial. Existing P11 pipeline doesn't specify interleaved sequencing → integration opportunity (Section 3, P18).

#### 2.5 Spaced Retrieval Practice — Optimal Gc Architecture

- **Spacing effect:** Cepeda et al. (2006) comprehensive meta-analysis — distributed practice dramatically outperforms massed practice for long-term retention; optimal spacing depends on desired retention interval. For long-term Gc: initial review intervals of 1–2 days, expanding to weeks/months as material consolidates, maximize retention vs any massed alternative. **Grade: HIGH**.
- **Testing effect / retrieval practice:** Roediger & Karpicke (2006) — self-testing >> rereading for long-term retention. Rowland (2014) meta-analysis confirms across conditions, mean **d ≈ 0.50** vs re-study controls. Mechanism: reconsolidation — each retrieval actively reconstructs and re-strengthens/elaborates the memory trace (not merely consolidation). **Grade: HIGH**.
- **System link:** existing +1/+5/+14 spaced surfacing schedule (P2, P14) instantiates spaced retrieval practice. RQ3 question — should this schedule differ by material type? → addressed by P17.

#### 2.6 Cross-Domain Transfer — Mechanisms and Conditions

**Transfer problem:** Near transfer (same problem type, slight variation) is relatively reliable; far transfer (structurally analogous but superficially different domain) is rare/difficult (Detterman & Sternberg, 1993).

**Conditions increasing far-transfer probability** (Grade: HIGH for conditions; MODERATE for specific implementations):
1. Abstract/deep-structure encoding (vs surface-procedure memorization) → recognizing structural analogies in new contexts
2. Multiple diverse examples of a schema across varied surface features → extracting abstract structure (Gick & Holyoak, 1983)
3. Explicit principle articulation after each example → greater transfer than non-articulators
4. Analogical comparison — presenting two structurally analogous problems from different domains and asking learners to map correspondence → forces abstraction of shared structure

**Breadth vs depth trade-off:** Most transfer-generative architecture = deep expertise in ≥1 domain (a well-structured "home base" schema for analogical transfer) + substantive exposure to multiple diverse domains (library of analogical cases). For a 7-subject 17-y/o: prioritize 1–2 anchor domains for expert-level schema-building; treat others as analogical/cross-domain enrichment sources.

#### 2.7 Vocabulary and Conceptual Network Development

- **Vocabulary as Gc:** Nation (2001) — reading comprehension requires knowledge of ~95–98% of running words; vocabulary gaps compound comprehension failures across exposure. **Grade: HIGH** (vocabulary-Gc relationship and acquisition-through-reading). Across 7 domains, systematic vocabulary development = high-leverage Gc investment.
- **Morphological awareness:** training in root/prefix/suffix decomposition produces vocabulary gains generalizing to novel words (Baumann et al., 2007). High-leverage for science: Greek/Latin morphemes (hydro-, thermo-, syn-, -ase, -oma, -genesis) recur across biology/chemistry/physics/medicine — morphological knowledge provides inference mechanism for novel technical vocabulary.

#### 2.8 Mental Models — Explicit Acquisition

- Johnson-Laird (1983): reasoning operates via constructing/manipulating mental models — internal representations preserving relational structure (not propositions, not images — schematic with spatial/dynamic structure). Superior reasoning ↔ ability to construct/manipulate accurate, high-fidelity models.
- **Explicit > implicit acquisition:** explicit model-building (diagramming causal structures, process flows, analogy maps, toy examples) produces more accurate/transferable models than reading-only acquisition (Chi et al., 1994; Schwartz & Bransford, 1998). **Grade: HIGH** (mental-model construction effects); **MODERATE** (specific explicit techniques). For physics/biology/chemistry: time drawing mechanism diagrams and worked-example causal chains is central to Gc development, not supplementary.

#### 2.9 Productive Failure and Desirable Difficulties

- **Kapur's Productive Failure** (2016): learners attempt problems *before* instruction. Attempts are imperfect/wrong, but the generation process activates/differentiates prior knowledge, preparing deeper benefit from subsequent instruction. Kapur's Singapore studies (incl. adolescents): problem-before-instruction students outperform direct-instruction-first students on transfer tests, even when worse on near-transfer. **Grade: HIGH** (adolescent populations studied); **MODERATE** (cross-domain generalizability).
- **Mechanism:** "preparation for future learning" (Schwartz & Bransford, 1998) — failure-generation creates a differentiated knowledge landscape (learner knows precisely what they don't know and what fails), enabling deeper encoding of subsequent canonical instruction. Distinct from discovery learning — instruction still occurs, but *after* the student generates/compares their own solutions.
- **System link:** existing P11 includes "problem-first sequencing." Productive failure literature provides design constraints (→ P19): problems must be genuinely complex (multiple competing sub-goals), generation phase long enough to exhaust obvious approaches, consolidation phase must explicitly compare generated approaches to canonical solution.

### Section 3: New Principles P17–P25

#### P17: Differentiated Retrieval Scheduling by Material Type

**Principle.** The uniform +1/+5/+14 spaced-surfacing schedule should differ by material type. Declarative Gc (facts, definitions, vocabulary, propositions) benefits from shorter initial intervals with rapid expansion once consolidated. Procedural/reasoning material (problem-solving schemas, math procedures, analytical frameworks) needs fewer repetitions if each repetition = retrieval-plus-application (not recognition), with intervals calibrated to schema complexity and including transfer-test items (novel problem instances), not repeats of familiar items.

**Implementation.** Tag notes by type: `[[declarative]]`, `[[procedural]]`, `[[conceptual-framework]]`.
| Type | Schedule | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Declarative | +1 / +5 / +14 / +30 / +90 | expanding interval after confirmed consolidation |
| Procedural | +2 / +10 / +30 | fewer reps, but novel transfer instances at each retrieval |
| Conceptual-framework | +1 / +7 / +21 | requires elaborative interrogation at each retrieval, not mere recognition |

**Classification heuristic:** Procedural = "can solve problems of type X / apply principle Y to novel situations." Declarative = "knows that Z is true / can define-recall X." Conceptual-Framework = "understands why Z is true and can apply across contexts." When uncertain, default Declarative for first cycle, reclassify based on actual retrieval struggles.

**Evidence:** Cepeda et al. (2006) optimal spacing ratios; declarative/procedural memory systems distinction (Squire, 1992); transfer-appropriate processing (Morris et al., 1977). **Grade: HIGH** (underlying principles); **MODERATE** (specific intervals).

**Relationship:** Modifies P2 (Spaced surfacing) and P14 (Three-retrieval scheduling).

#### P18: Interleaved Session Architecture

**Principle.** Study sessions interleave problem types and (where possible) subject domains within a session, rather than blocking. Applies to practice sets, active-retrieval sessions, and sequencing of Consolidate-phase activities across subjects within a day.

**Implementation — "Interleave Gate":** No more than 25–30 minutes of blocked practice on one problem type before switching to a different type/domain. Session plans built with explicit interleaving.
- Within-subject example (AP Physics): 3 kinematics problems → 3 electrostatics → 3 thermodynamics → return to kinematics
- Cross-subject example: AP Physics problem set → AP Biology concept retrieval → AP Chemistry mechanism diagram → Mathematics proof attempt → return to AP Physics

**Exception:** Deep generative writing sessions (drafting explanations, conceptual maps) benefit from sustained attention — do not interleave at session level, though subjects can rotate across sessions.

**Evidence:** Kornell & Bjork (2008); Rohrer et al. (2014); Taylor & Rohrer (2010). **Grade: HIGH**.

**Relationship:** New principle, no direct predecessor. Augments P11 (Problem-first sequencing) with session-level architecture. Adds beneficial friction, consistent with P8 (Friction budgets).

#### P19: Productive Failure Problem Design Protocol

**Principle.** The "Attempt" phase of P11 should be explicitly designed as a productive-failure episode when material is new/conceptually rich — meeting Kapur's criteria: complex enough for multiple competing approaches, open enough for wrong-but-reasonable attempts, immediately followed by structured comparison.

**Implementation.** Tag new first-encounter "Attempt" prompts `[[productive-failure-mode]]`:
1. Present a problem requiring the target concept, for which the student lacks the canonical method.
2. Require ≥2 distinct solution attempts — including at least one the student can identify as flawed and explain why.
3. No hints during generation unless ≥15 minutes elapse with no productive generation.
4. In Consolidate, explicitly compare generated approaches to canonical solution, identifying which elements captured genuine structural features.

**Evidence:** Kapur (2016); Schwartz & Bransford (1998); DeCaro & Rittle-Johnson (2012). **Grade: HIGH** (Kapur's adolescent studies); **MODERATE** (implementation details).

**Relationship:** Refines P11 (Problem-first sequencing) and P12 (Socratic-first AI interaction) — during productive-failure episodes, P12's Socratic restraint extends to: AI asks "What approaches have you tried and why do you think they're not working?" before any conceptual scaffolding. Activates P5 (Metacognitive scaffolding) via explicit prediction-then-compare.

#### P20: Cross-Domain Analogical Mapping Trigger

**Principle.** When a concept is consolidated in one domain, the system prompts an explicit analogical mapping to ≥1 structurally related concept in a different domain — student generates the mapping *before* the AI reveals it. Specific application of the "multiple diverse examples + explicit principle articulation" transfer condition (Section 2.6).

**Implementation — "Analogy Gate":** After a concept passes +14 retrieval without errors, AI prompts: "This concept has a structural analog in [domain X]. Can you propose what it might be and describe the structural mapping?"

Canonical examples:
- Enzyme kinetics (AP Biology) ↔ catalysis rate equations (AP Chemistry) ↔ activation energy in learning (Neuroscience)
- Gradient descent (Machine Learning) ↔ natural selection (AP Biology) ↔ market equilibration (Marketing)
- Osmotic pressure (AP Biology/Chemistry) ↔ diffusion potentials (Neuroscience) ↔ entropy gradients (AP Physics)

Sequence: student generates analogy → AI reveals canonical structural mapping → student identifies what their mapping captured vs missed.

**Evidence:** Gick & Holyoak (1983); Gentner & Markman (1997); Novick (1988). **Grade: HIGH** (analogical reasoning effects); **MODERATE** (specific implementation protocol).

**Relationship:** New principle extending P4 (Generation before suggestion) into cross-domain transfer. Works with P16's narrative approach by supplying ready-made analogical bridges for the narrative layer.

---

#### P21: Dual-Process Metacognitive Calibration

**Principle.** P5 (Metacognitive scaffolding) and P15 (Desirable difficulty calibration) don't distinguish Type 1 (fast/intuitive/automatic) vs Type 2 (slow/deliberate/effortful) processing in calibration. Fluency (ease information comes to mind) is an unreliable calibration signal — reflects familiarity, not understanding. Calibration should probe understanding specifically.

**Implementation.** Add a third rating dimension to existing Difficulty (1–5) / Fluency (1–5): **Understanding (1–5)**:

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| U1 | Can reproduce the information but cannot explain why it is true |
| U2 | Can explain the what and some of the why, but not the mechanism |
| U3 | Can explain mechanism, but only in the studied context |
| U4 | Can generate a novel example and identify limits of the principle |
| U5 | Can spot cases where the principle breaks down and explain what corrects it |

Sessions where Fluency is high but Understanding < U3 → flagged for elaborative-interrogation retrieval (not spaced extension). Prevents the "illusion of knowing" (Glenberg et al., 1982) — spaced repetition producing confident-but-shallow Gc.

**Evidence:** Bjork et al. (2013) on metacognitive monitoring; Koriat & Bjork (2005) on fluency-understanding dissociation; Evans & Stanovich (2013) on dual-process theory. **Grade: HIGH** (fluency-understanding dissociation); **MODERATE** (specific U-scale implementation).

**Relationship:** Extends P5 and P15; modifies the daily rating system; compatible with P1 (Retrieval-first).

---

#### P22: Aerobic Exercise — Cognitive Priming Protocol

**Principle.** Acute aerobic exercise (20–30 min, 60–75% max HR) produces measurable executive-function enhancement lasting 30–60 min post-exercise. System should track exercise timing and sequence demanding cognitive sessions into this window.

**Implementation.** Exercise log fields: `date`, `type`, `duration`, `intensity (1–3)`, `time_completed`. When scheduling: "You exercised at [time]. Your optimal cognitive window is approximately [time+30min] to [time+90min]. Recommend scheduling [most demanding cognitive task] in this window." Scheduling heuristic, not rigid rule (strict compliance would violate P8).

**Evidence:** Chang et al. (2012); Hillman et al. (2009); Lambourne & Tomporowski (2010) (pre- vs during-exercise distinction). **Grade: MODERATE** (acute effects well-established; long-term architecture effects more uncertain).

**Relationship:** New principle outside existing scope. Interfaces with P6 (State-aware offloading) by adding physiological state (post-exercise) to the state-awareness framework.

---

#### P23: Sleep Architecture Protection Protocol

**Principle.** Support protection of both SWS (early-night) and REM (late-night) phases, with attention to adolescent circadian delay — track bedtime/wake time, flag REM-truncation risk on early-wake plans, note late-night screen exposure likely to suppress melatonin onset.

**Implementation.** Daily log: `sleep_target`, `actual_sleep`, `wake_time`, `pre_sleep_screen (Y/N)`. Weekly summary includes:
- "REM Protection Score" — flags nights with total sleep <8 hrs
- "Circadian Risk" flag — screen exposure within 60 min of target bedtime
- Cognitive session records tagged with prior-night sleep quality; 2 consecutive low-sleep days before a session → AI notes possible impaired performance/consolidation

**Evidence:** Walker & Stickgold (2004) on REM and relational memory; Tononi & Cirelli (2014) on SWS and synaptic homeostasis; Carskadon et al. (1993) on adolescent circadian delay. **Grade: HIGH** (mechanisms); **MODERATE** (tracking implementation).

**Relationship:** New principle extending P6 (State-aware offloading) with a sleep-state dimension.

---

#### P24: Elaborative Interrogation Protocol for Consolidation Phase

**Principle.** The Consolidate phase of P11 should include an elaborative-interrogation step: for each major new fact/principle, the student writes an answer to "Why is this true?" before reviewing source material. Distinct from retrieval practice (tests *what*) and self-explanation (explains worked examples) — targets the connection between new information and prior schema.

**Implementation.** Before writing the vault note, answer three AI-generated prompts:
1. "Why is [new concept] the case?" (causal mechanism)
2. "What existing concept in [related domain] does this remind you of, and why?"
3. "What would a world look like in which [new concept] were false?" (counterfactual elaboration — forces identification of boundary conditions)

**Evidence:** Pressley et al. (1987); Willingham (2004); Seifert (1993). **Grade: HIGH** (standard elaborative interrogation); **MODERATE** (counterfactual extension).

**Relationship:** Refines P11's Consolidate phase. Compatible with P13 (Generation-before-vault) — elaborative-interrogation outputs generated before vault notes, ensuring vaulted material reflects genuine constructed knowledge.

---

#### P25: Schema Externalization and Mental Model Audit

**Principle.** For each major conceptual domain, maintain an externalized schema map — visual/structured representation of knowledge architecture — explicitly compared against expert representations monthly. Comparison identifies gaps, misattributions, missing relational links.

**Implementation.** Per subject, maintain `[[schema-map-[subject]]]` (Obsidian Canvas or structured link hierarchy) showing major concepts, relationships, consolidation level (color-coded by P21 U-rating). Monthly cycle:
1. Student reviews schema map, identifies 3 least-confident links.
2. AI presents 3 targeted retrieval challenges on those links.
3. Student compares schema map to a structured expert outline (textbook/syllabus), identifies what's present in expert representation but absent in theirs.
4. Gaps become "Gap Notes" tagged `[[to-consolidate]]`, entering the P17 retrieval schedule.

**Evidence:** Chi et al. (1981) on expert-novice knowledge structure; Novak & Cañas (2006) on concept mapping (**d ≈ 0.36** vs lecture alone); Schwartz & Bransford (1998) on differentiated knowledge. **Grade: MODERATE** (concept mapping); **HIGH** (expert-novice schema comparison mechanism).

**Relationship:** New principle synthesizing P5 (Metacognitive scaffolding), P4 (Generation before suggestion), P2 (Spaced surfacing). Provides a persistent externalized metacognitive object the existing system lacks.

### Section 4: External Practices (Outside System Scope)

Priority order = effect × tractability × adolescent-specificity.

#### Priority 1: Aerobic Exercise Protocol
- 4–5 sessions/week, sustained aerobic activity
- 25–35 min/session at 60–75% max HR (conversation possible but difficult)
- Schedule hardest study session 30–60 min after exercise
- Any sustained modality (running, cycling, swimming, rowing; team sports count if sustained aerobic load maintained)
- Year-round consistency > intensity — chronic moderate activity produces cumulative structural benefits acute intense exercise doesn't
- **Grade: HIGH** (executive function) / **MODERATE** (Gf specifically)

#### Priority 2: Sleep Architecture Optimization
- Target: 8.5–9.5 hrs in bed/night (accounting for onset latency)
- Bedtime 10:30–11:00 PM; wake 7:00–8:00 AM (preserves REM-rich late sleep)
- Screen dimming/blue-light blocking starting 60 min before bedtime (Gooley et al., 2011)
- Weekend consistency: ≤60–90 min past weekday wake time (social jet lag degrades weekday sleep)
- No caffeine after 2 PM (half-life ~5–6 hrs)
- **Grade: HIGH** (consolidation mechanisms; adolescent circadian biology)

#### Priority 3: Social Cognitive Engagement
- **Protégé effect:** Nestojko et al. (2014) — expecting to teach improves learning more than expecting a test, even without actually teaching; actual teaching shows stronger effects (Cohen et al., 1982 meta-analysis: **d ≈ 0.55**). **Grade: HIGH**.
- Protocol: explain ≥1 concept/week to another person without reference materials; ≥1 collaborative problem-solving session/week on genuinely difficult material; consider peer-tutoring younger students in advanced-consolidation subjects (solidifies own schema + protégé effect).

#### Priority 4: Nutrition — BDNF-Supporting Pattern
- Omega-3 (DHA/EPA): 2–3 servings fatty fish/week or 1000–2000 mg DHA+EPA/day supplementation — DHA is a structural component of neuronal membranes, supports synaptic plasticity (Horrocks & Yeo, 1999). **Grade: MODERATE/TRANSFERRED**.
- Consistent meal timing: skipping breakfast → degraded morning cognition in adolescents (Grantham-McGregor, 2005). **Grade: MODERATE**.
- Adequate iron: deficiency (common in adolescents, esp. restricted diets) → impaired attention/cognition; threshold effect, no benefit above sufficiency (Bruner et al., 1996). **Grade: HIGH** (deficiency effects).
- Hydration: even mild dehydration (1–2% body mass) degrades cognition (Adan, 2012). **Grade: MODERATE**.
- **No evidence:** nootropic stacks, racetams, smart drugs — no reliable benefit in healthy adolescents, not recommended.

#### Priority 5: Stress Management and Cortisol Control
Chronic elevated cortisol is neurotoxic to hippocampus (McEwen, 2007), degrades working memory (Lupien et al., 2007), impairs PFC.
- Aerobic exercise (Priority 1) = most reliable acute/chronic stress reducer
- Social support buffers cortisol response to stressors (Eisenberger et al., 2007)
- Cognitive reappraisal of stress arousal as enhancement (not impairment) improves cognitive task performance (Jamieson et al., 2010). **Grade: MODERATE**.
- Avoid acute over-cramming — massed high-stress study produces worse consolidation (cortisol-hippocampus interaction undermines consolidation)

#### Priority 6: Attentional Environment Management
- Heavy passive social media use (>3 hrs/day) associated with degraded attentional control in adolescents (Wilmer et al., 2017; Loh & Kanai, 2016). **Grade: MODERATE** (correlational).
- Phone physically out of sight during study — mere presence on desk degrades available working memory even unused (Ward et al., 2017). **Grade: MODERATE**.
- Deep-work sessions (60–90 min uninterrupted) incompatible with notification-checking — refocus cost after interruption ≈ 15–20 min (Mark et al., 2008). **Grade: MODERATE**.

### Section 5: Priority Stack

*Caveat: all effect sizes are population averages; individual response varies by genetics, baseline, adherence — treat as hypotheses to test against own data.*

| Rank | Action | Effect | Tractability | Adolescent-Specificity | Immediate Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sleep Architecture Optimization (8.5+ hrs, protect REM) | Large (mechanism-level — affects consolidation of everything else) | Moderate (free but requires sustained commitment) | Very High (SWS abundance is adolescent-specific; REM truncation risk highest when sleep need is highest) | Bedtime 10:45 PM, wake 7:30 AM, screen dimming from 10:00 PM. 2 weeks before evaluating session quality. |
| 2 | Aerobic Exercise Pre-Study Protocol (4x/week, hardest study 30–45 min post-exercise) | Moderate–High (exec. function; elevated adolescent BDNF response) | High (no equipment needed; 30 min 4x/week) | High (elevated BDNF; coincides with PFC myelination; structural effects more plausible than adulthood) | Implement 4x/week aerobic sessions; schedule hardest study block 30–45 min after each. |
| 3 | P17 + P18 (Differentiated Retrieval Scheduling + Interleaved Sessions) | High (interleaving + retrieval practice among most robust learning-science effects) | High (system modification only) | Moderate (effects hold across ages; specificity from volume of new learning needing proper consolidation architecture) | Audit/tag current notes by material type (declarative/procedural/conceptual-framework); redesign next week's schedule with explicit interleaving. |
| 4 | Productive Failure Protocol for New Concepts (P19) | High for Gf-adjacent pattern extraction/transfer; Kapur's adolescent data directly applicable | Moderate (difficulty is psychological, not logistical) | High (coincides with peak pruning period; high-demand generation may contribute to circuit selection) | Apply full productive-failure protocol to next 3 new concepts before consulting sources; track P21 Understanding ratings before/after. |
| 5 | Elaborative Interrogation + Analogy Gate (P24 + P20) | High (elaborative interrogation d ≈ 0.60; reliable analogical transfer under right conditions) | High (prompt modification to existing Consolidate phase only) | Moderate (effects robust across ages; specificity from multi-domain breadth of curriculum) | After each Consolidate phase this week, answer the 3 elaborative-interrogation prompts; after next +14 retrieval on any consolidated concept, add Analogy Gate prompt. |

Why this order: Rank 1 (sleep) underlies consolidation of every other investment. Rank 2 (exercise) is both an acute primer and long-term structural investment. Rank 3 (P17+P18) compounds on the existing +1/+5/+14 foundation. Rank 4 (P19) formalizes the existing problem-first instinct into a precision tool. Rank 5 (P24+P20) converts the 7-subject curriculum's parallel domain knowledge into an integrated, transfer-enabling schema network.

### Discussion and Limitations

**Discussion.** The 12–25 neuroplasticity window is real (ongoing pruning, incomplete PFC myelination, elevated BDNF sensitivity, active experience-dependent circuit selection) and creates a window where high-cognitive-demand experience may shape adult cognitive architecture. But it is not magic: no intervention program lets a 17-year-old substantially raise their adult Gf ceiling — adult Gf heritability remains the dominant constraint on outcome variance. The window offers somewhat greater plasticity, hence somewhat greater response to well-designed inputs, not unlimited potential.

The strongest levers (sleep, aerobic exercise) are not cognitively exciting and cannot be purchased or system-prompted — they require sustained behavioral commitment against adolescent social norms (late nights, sedentary screen time). The gap between knowing the evidence and doing the behavior is the real challenge, not a research question.

The existing 16-principle system already correctly implements retrieval practice, Socratic scaffolding, metacognitive calibration, generation-before-vault — P17–P25 are genuine extensions, not error corrections. The most significant gaps filled: (1) no differentiated scheduling by material type (P17), (2) no cross-domain analogical transfer architecture (P20), (3) no externalized schema audit (P25).

Bottom line: a Gf-constrained learner with expert Gc outperforms an unconstrained learner with shallow Gc in most knowledge-rich domains. Gf is the engine; Gc is the fuel. Maximizing Gc while the engine develops is the dominant lever, not a consolation prize.

**Limitations.**
1. **Adult-sample extrapolation** — most learning-strategy studies use college/adult populations; direct adolescent evidence is sparser than desirable.
2. **Far-transfer inconsistency** — many positive findings are near-transfer/domain-specific and may not generalize to standard-test Gf. Far transfer remains the most elusive cognitive-training outcome.
3. **Self-selection bias** — music training, bilingualism, advanced coursework, and exercise studies all face the confound that engaged individuals differ systematically from non-engaged individuals.
4. **Effect size inflation** — publication bias systematically inflates published effects; true effects are probably smaller. The priority stack relies on relative ranking, not absolute effect-size estimates.
5. **Individual differences** — recommendations are group-level; individual response varies substantially. Protocols are hypotheses to test against the learner's own data (which P15/P21 are positioned to collect).
6. **P17–P25 are partially speculative** — underlying evidence is real; specific implementation parameters are evidence-informed approximations, not experimentally validated as a system.
