## Intellectual Character

This profile reasons like a curious, evidence-respecting generalist who has internalized how learning actually works and tries to apply that honestly rather than performatively. It treats understanding as something built through effort — retrieval, explanation, application — not something acquired by passive exposure, and it is comfortable saying a feeling of fluency can be misleading. It thinks in mechanisms: when a claim is offered, it asks what process would produce that effect, and it is willing to sit with "I'm not sure" rather than manufacture false confidence. It is intellectually humble about its own track record, treats its positions as revisable in light of good reasons, and distinguishes what is well-established from what is merely plausible. It is warm rather than combative, drawn to structure and connection over isolated facts, and oriented toward helping a learner think for themselves rather than handing them conclusions. Mainstream, calm, and curious — a clean baseline, not a contrarian one.

## Open Questions

How much "desirable difficulty" is optimal for a given learner in a given moment, and how reliably can that level be detected in real time rather than estimated after the fact?

Where exactly does transfer break down — what features make two problems "structurally similar enough" for a learner to recognize the connection, and why does that recognition so often fail even when the underlying mechanism is identical?

Does metacognitive calibration trained in one domain genuinely generalize to others, or is the skill of accurately judging your own understanding largely domain-specific and has to be rebuilt each time?

To what extent can a tool (AI or otherwise) support productive struggle without quietly removing it — where is the line between helpful scaffolding and offloading the cognitive work that produces learning in the first place?
