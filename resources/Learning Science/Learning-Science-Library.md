## DOMAIN 1: Retrieval Practice & the Testing Effect
> Taking a memory test isn't just assessment — it's one of the most reliable ways to make memory durable. This is the empirical spine of the vault's "retrieval before reveal" principle.

**Test-Enhanced Learning: Taking Memory Tests Improves Long-Term Retention**
- Author / source: Roediger, H. L. & Karpicke, J. D. (2006), *Psychological Science*, 17, 249–255
- URL: https://colinallen.dnsalias.org/Readings/2006_Roediger_Karpicke_PsychSci.pdf (mirror: http://psychnet.wustl.edu/memory/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/Roediger-Karpicke-2006_PPS.pdf)
- What it covers: The foundational experiment showing repeated testing beats repeated restudying for long-term retention, even though restudying feels more productive in the moment.
- Why it's here: This is the single paper the vault's "generation before reveal" and spaced-retrieval scheduling rest on.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Repeated Retrieval During Learning Is the Key to Long-Term Retention**
- Author / source: Karpicke, J. D. & Roediger, H. L. (2007), *Journal of Memory and Language*, 57, 151–162
- URL: https://learninglab.psych.purdue.edu/downloads/2007/2007_Karpicke_Roediger_JML.pdf
- What it covers: Follow-up study isolating *repeated* retrieval (vs. single-test) as the active ingredient, and showing dropping items once "known" hurts retention.
- Why it's here: Directly grounds the system's "don't stop reviewing just because recall succeeded once" scheduling logic.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**What Works, What Doesn't: Improving Students' Learning with Effective Learning Techniques**
- Author / source: Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013), *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, 14(1), 4–58
- URL: https://www.psychologicalscience.org/publications/journals/pspi/learning-techniques.html (free PDF mirror: https://pcl.sitehost.iu.edu/rgoldsto/courses/dunloskyimprovinglearning.pdf)
- What it covers: A systematic review ranking ten common study techniques (practice testing and distributed practice rated highest-utility; highlighting and rereading rated lowest) by evidence quality and generalizability.
- Why it's here: The single best "what actually works" review in the field — directly justifies why this system prioritizes retrieval and spacing over highlighting/rereading.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning**
- Author / source: Brown, P. C., Roediger, H. L., & McDaniel, M. A. (2014), Belknap Press
- URL: https://www.makeitstick.net/ (official site/summary); book itself is not freely hosted
- What it covers: Trade-press synthesis of retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, and desirable difficulties, written for a general audience with concrete study tactics.
- Why it's here: The most widely-read accessible entry point into this entire literature — good first read before the primary papers.
- Accessibility: [MARKDOWN] [NOTEBOOKLM]

## DOMAIN 2: Spacing & Interleaving
> Spreading practice out over time, and mixing problem types instead of blocking them, both feel worse during learning and produce better long-term retention and transfer than the "smooth" alternative.

**Learning Concepts and Categories: Is Spacing the "Enemy of Induction"?**
- Author / source: Kornell, N. & Bjork, R. A. (2008), *Psychological Science*, 19, 585–592
- URL: https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/Kornell_Bjork_2008_PsychScience.pdf
- What it covers: Shows spaced (vs. massed) exposure to examples improves induction of underlying categories/rules, even though learners rate massed study as more effective.
- Why it's here: Grounds the system's "Analogy Gate" and interleaved-session architecture (Principle 17–18).
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Why Interleaving Enhances Inductive Learning: The Roles of Discrimination and Retrieval**
- Author / source: Birnbaum, M. S., Kornell, N., Bjork, E. L., & Bjork, R. A. (2013), *Memory & Cognition*, 41, 392–402
- URL: https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/Birnbaum_Kornell_EBjork_RBjork_inpress.pdf
- What it covers: Mechanistic follow-up isolating *why* interleaving works — discrimination between categories plus spaced retrieval, not just variability.
- Why it's here: Explains the mechanism behind interleaved practice, not just the effect — useful for designing session structure correctly.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Interleaved Practice Improves Mathematics Learning**
- Author / source: Rohrer, D., Dedrick, R. F., & Stershic, S. (2015), *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 107(3), 900–908
- URL: http://uweb.cas.usf.edu/~drohrer/pdfs/Rohrer_et_al_2015JEdPsych.pdf
- What it covers: Classroom randomized-controlled trial (the gold standard) showing interleaved math homework outperforms blocked homework on a later unannounced test.
- Why it's here: One of the few interleaving studies run in a real classroom rather than a lab — strong external validity for the vault's session-design principle.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**The Effects of Interleaved Practice**
- Author / source: Taylor, K. & Rohrer, D. (2010), *Applied Cognitive Psychology*, 24, 837–848
- URL: https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/2014-rohrer.pdf (related Rohrer mirror via gwern.net doc archive)
- What it covers: Shows interleaved (vs. blocked) practice on different problem *types* improves students' ability to discriminate which strategy a new problem requires — the actual bottleneck in math transfer.
- Why it's here: Directly supports why the system mixes topics across sessions rather than batching by topic.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Doug Rohrer — Publications Page**
- Author / source: Rohrer, D. (University of South Florida)
- URL: http://drohrer.myweb.usf.edu/pubs.htm
- What it covers: Free-PDF archive of Rohrer's full interleaving/spacing publication list, continually updated.
- Why it's here: One-stop hub if deeper or more recent interleaving studies are needed later.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

## DOMAIN 3: Cognitive Load & Working Memory
> Working memory is the bottleneck of learning. Instruction (and self-directed study) succeeds or fails depending on whether it manages intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load — not on how much "content" is delivered.

**Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on Learning**
- Author / source: Sweller, J. (1988), *Cognitive Science*, 12(2), 257–285
- URL: https://andymatuschak.org/files/papers/Sweller%20-%201988%20-%20Cognitive%20load%20during%20problem%20solving.pdf
- What it covers: The original cognitive load theory paper — introduces intrinsic vs. extraneous load and shows means-ends problem solving (e.g., conventional "solve for X") consumes working-memory capacity that could otherwise go toward schema acquisition.
- Why it's here: The foundational paper behind the system's "germane vs. extraneous friction budget" principle.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Cognitive Load Theory (notes)**
- Author / source: Matuschak, A.
- URL: https://notes.andymatuschak.org/Cognitive_load_theory
- What it covers: A concise, linked, continuously-updated synthesis of CLT — intrinsic/extraneous/germane load, the modality and split-attention effects, expertise reversal — written for practitioners building learning tools.
- Why it's here: A fast, free, accessible on-ramp to CLT before tackling Sweller's original paper.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Working Memory (Baddeley's multi-component model)**
- Author / source: Baddeley, A. D. — overview via Simply Psychology
- URL: https://www.simplypsychology.org/working%20memory.html
- What it covers: Accessible explainer of the phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, episodic buffer, and central executive — the architecture cognitive load theory operates on.
- Why it's here: Background needed to understand *why* extraneous load is so costly — working memory's severe capacity limit.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

## DOMAIN 4: Desirable Difficulties, Productive Failure & the Generation Effect

- URL: https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/RBjork_inpress.pdf
- What it covers: Bjork's own statement of the desirable-difficulties framework — why conditions that slow apparent learning often improve long-term retention and transfer, and the conditions under which difficulty becomes simply "undesirable."
- Why it's here: The canonical, directly-from-source statement of the principle the vault calls "productive friction."
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Bjork Learning and Forgetting Lab — Research Page**

- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**The Generation Effect: Delineation of a Phenomenon**

## DOMAIN 5: Metacognition, Calibration & the Fluency Illusion
> Learners are bad judges of their own learning — fluency during study is mistaken for durable understanding. Calibration (knowing what you don't know) has to be engineered, not assumed.

**Illusions of Competence in Monitoring One's Knowledge During Study**
- Author / source: Koriat, A. & Bjork, R. A. (2005), *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 31, 187–194
- URL: https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/Koriat_RBjork_2005.pdf
- What it covers: Identifies "foresight bias" — the mismatch between conditions during study (answer visible) and at test (answer must be retrieved) that causes learners to systematically overestimate what they'll remember.
- Why it's here: This is the direct empirical basis for the vault's rule that self-report confidence is unreliable and must be replaced by objective retrieval checks (the ≥20-point calibration-gap signal).
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Self-Regulated Learning: Beliefs, Techniques, and Illusions**
- Author / source: Bjork, R. A., Dunlosky, J., & Kornell, N. (2013), *Annual Review of Psychology*, 64, 417–444
- URL: https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/Bjork_Dunlosky_Kornell_2013.pdf
- What it covers: Comprehensive review of how learners regulate (and mis-regulate) their own study — covers judgments of learning, the labor-in-vain effect, and why expert-feeling fluency misleads novices.
- Why it's here: One of the most-cited reviews underlying Principle 19 (dual-process metacognitive calibration, the U1–U5 scale).
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Thinking, Fast and Slow**
- Author / source: Kahneman, D. (2011), Farrar, Straus and Giroux
- URL: https://archive.org/details/thinkingfastslow0000kahn (Internet Archive — borrowing/preview only; not a free download)
- What it covers: The popular synthesis of dual-process theory (System 1 / System 2) — fast, automatic, fluency-driven judgment vs. slow, effortful, deliberate reasoning.
- Why it's here: The accessible trade-book version of the dual-process framework that Evans & Stanovich (2013) formalize academically — background for why fluency illusions happen at all.
- Accessibility: [MARKDOWN] [NOTEBOOKLM]

**Dual-Process Theories of Higher Cognition: Advancing the Debate**
- Author / source: Evans, J. St. B. T. & Stanovich, K. E. (2013), *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 8(3), 223–241
- URL: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691612460685
- What it covers: The academic, more rigorous formalization of Type 1 (fast/intuitive) vs. Type 2 (slow/deliberate) processing, clarifying common misconceptions about the dual-process framework.
- Why it's here: The precise academic grounding (vs. Kahneman's popularization) for the system's dual-process calibration principle.

## DOMAIN 6: Transfer & Analogical Reasoning
> Knowledge that stays locked to the context it was learned in is far less useful than knowledge that transfers. Transfer doesn't happen automatically — it requires deliberately abstracting the shared structure across multiple examples.

**Schema Induction and Analogical Transfer**
- Author / source: Gick, M. L. & Holyoak, K. J. (1983), *Cognitive Psychology*, 15, 1–38
- URL: https://reasoninglab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/273/2021/04/Gick_Holyoak1983_SchemaInduction.pdf
- What it covers: The classic "radiation problem" / "fortress problem" studies showing a single analogous example rarely transfers spontaneously, but comparing *two* analogous examples reliably induces a transferable schema.
- Why it's here: The direct empirical basis for the system's "Analogy Gate" — why the system asks for more than one example before claiming a principle is generalized.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Structure Mapping in Analogy and Similarity**
- Author / source: Gentner, D. & Markman, A. B. (1997), *American Psychologist*, 52(1), 45–56
- URL: https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/GentnerMarkman97.pdf
- What it covers: Structure-mapping theory — analogical reasoning works by aligning relational structure between domains, not surface features; explains why surface-similar analogies often mislead.
- Why it's here: Explains *why* the Analogy Gate must check relational structure, not surface resemblance, before accepting a cross-domain mapping.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Analogical Transfer, Problem Similarity, and Expertise**
- Author / source: Novick, L. R. (1988), *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 14(3), 510–520
- URL: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/19656632_Analogical_Transfer_Problem_Similarity_and_Expertise
- What it covers: Shows novices are misled by surface-similar-but-structurally-different problems ("negative transfer"), while experts are better able to ignore surface features and use structural similarity.
- Why it's here: Grounds the system's expertise-graded defaults — novices need more scaffolding against false-analogy traps than experts do.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

## DOMAIN 8: Attention, Open Loops & Cognitive State
> Attention has structure: it lingers on unfinished tasks (attention residue), it wanders during low-stimulation states (default mode network), and it can be deliberately closed out or deliberately released. Working *with* this architecture beats fighting it.

- Why it's here: The direct empirical source for this vault's note on the Zeigarnik Effect and Attention Residue.

- Why it's here: Up-to-date academic grounding for the vault's Default Mode Network and Boredom note, replacing reliance on older/looser popular accounts.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

## DOMAIN 10: Abstraction & Conceptual Structure
> How minds choose levels of description, compress structure into transferable form, and decide which "frame" or representation best fits a problem. This is the conceptual substrate beneath transfer, schema-building, and analogical reasoning — less settled empirically than the other domains, drawing heavily on philosophy of mind/science and computational theories of cognition rather than randomized experiments.

**Framework synthesis** *(the conceptual model this domain section is built around)*
- **Abstraction is epistemic compression, not a claim about what exists.** A "level of description" tracks the complexity or scale a finite mind can hold at once — it does not mark where a pattern actually lives. A pattern that requires many interacting components (a traffic jam, a population under selection) is fully present at the fine-grained, base level; calling it a separate "higher level" is a statement about a limited observer's capacity, not about an extra layer of reality.
- **The best frame for a problem is the one that preserves maximum task-relevant structure per unit of working-memory (or representational) cost it imposes.** This criterion is purpose-relative (it depends on what the task needs) and capacity-relative (it depends on what the reasoner can actually hold and manipulate). There is no single "right" level of abstraction in the abstract — only an optimal frame for a given (goal, capacity) pair.
- **Capacity and framing re-entangle at the high end.** Because the best affordable frame is the most powerful one a reasoner can run, more cognitive capacity expands the set of frames that become affordable — granting access to more fine-grained, more structurally faithful representations that a less capacious mind must compress away. Expertise and unusual insight often look like access to a better frame, not more raw processing power in the abstract.
- **Cross-substrate sameness (the same pattern recurring in different physical systems — multiple realizability) is itself a relation, not evidence of an extra ontological layer.** Two systems sharing a structure (e.g., natural selection occurring in biological populations and in software) are related by an isomorphism a sufficiently complete description would simply note; naming the shared pattern is still a compression made by and for a limited reasoner, not the discovery of a freestanding "higher-level" object.
- **Working within this view, "thinking well" is choosing the compression that keeps the structure a problem needs while staying inside the reasoner's capacity** — and deliberately moving to a different grain (finer, coarser, or laterally to a different conceptual branch) when the current frame hides what the problem requires.
- This is a coherent interpretive framework, not a consensus position — it leans on one contestable premise (that higher-level laws/generalizations are compressions rather than independently real, irreducible features), which is the live reductionism-vs-non-reductive-physicalism debate in philosophy of science.

**Structure-Mapping: A Theoretical Framework for Analogy**
- Author / source: Gentner, D. (1983), *Cognitive Science*, 7(2), 155–170
- URL: https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/Gentner83.2b.pdf
- What it covers: The founding structure-mapping theory — analogy and abstraction work by aligning relational structure between a base and target domain, not by matching surface features; introduces the systematicity principle for evaluating which structural mappings are worth keeping.
- Why it's here: The clearest formal account of how relational structure (rather than surface content) gets extracted and carried across representations — directly underlies the "abstraction = preserved structure" claim in the framework synthesis above. Cross-listed conceptually with Domain 6.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Marr's Levels of Analysis (computational / algorithmic / implementational)**
- Author / source: Marr, D. (1982), *Vision: A Computational Investigation into the Human Representation and Processing of Visual Information*, MIT Press — summarized via Matuschak's notes
- URL: https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zQnTUMfm4YPLyi8GFA1Ym1e
- What it covers: Marr's three-level framework for analyzing any information-processing system — the computational level (what problem is being solved), the algorithmic level (what procedure solves it), and the implementational level (how it's physically realized) — and why conflating levels causes confused explanations.
- Why it's here: A canonical, formal demonstration that the "right" level of description depends on the explanatory question being asked — the clearest precedent for the purpose-relative half of the best-frame criterion.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Real Patterns**
- Author / source: Dennett, D. C. (1991), *The Journal of Philosophy*, 88(1), 27–51
- URL: https://web.ics.purdue.edu/~drkelly/DCDRealPatterns1991.pdf
- What it covers: Argues that higher-level patterns (e.g., propositional-attitude descriptions of behavior) can be real and predictively useful without being reducible to a single privileged physical description — a "mild realism" about patterns that exist relative to a noise floor and an observer's compression strategy.
- Why it's here: The most direct philosophical precedent for treating levels/patterns as real-but-epistemic — a pattern can be a genuine, exploitable regularity while still being a compression rather than an additional ontological layer.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Emergent Properties**
- Author / source: O'Connor, T. & Wong, H. Y., *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*
- URL: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/properties-emergent/
- What it covers: Survey of philosophical accounts of emergence — weak/epistemic emergence (novel only relative to a predictive or descriptive method) vs. strong/ontological emergence (genuinely irreducible, with new causal powers), and the arguments for and against each.
- Why it's here: Supplies the precise vocabulary (weak vs. strong emergence) for the framework synthesis's claim that ordinary "higher-level" phenomena are weakly/epistemically emergent rather than ontologically novel.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Multiple Realizability**
- Author / source: Bickle, J., *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*
- URL: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/multiple-realizability/
- What it covers: The thesis that a single higher-level kind (a mental state, a biological trait, a software process) can be realized by many distinct lower-level physical configurations, and what this does and doesn't imply about whether higher-level kinds are reducible.
- Why it's here: Direct grounding for the framework's treatment of cross-substrate sameness (e.g., selection in biology and in software) as an isomorphism relation rather than evidence for an independent higher-level entity.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Reductionism in Biology**
- Author / source: Sarkar, S. & Plutynski, A. (eds. entry), *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*
- URL: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/reduction-biology/
- What it covers: Surveys the reductionism vs. non-reductive-physicalism debate as it plays out in biology, including whether multiple realizability blocks theory reduction and whether higher-level biological generalizations (e.g., evolutionary or ecological laws) are dispensable compressions or irreducible explanatory tools.
- Why it's here: Direct source for the framework's acknowledged "load-bearing plank" — whether higher-level laws are real-and-irreducible (Fodor's special-sciences argument) or compressions a bandwidth-limited mind uses. Presents both sides rather than assuming the framework's answer.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]

**Good Regulator Theorem (Conant & Ashby, 1970)**
- Author / source: overview via Wikipedia, summarizing Conant, R. C. & Ashby, W. R. (1970), *International Journal of Systems Science*, 1(2), 89–97
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_regulator_theorem
- What it covers: The formal result that an optimally simple controller/regulator of a system must function as a model (homomorphic image) of that system — i.e., effective control requires holding a compressed but structurally faithful internal model.
- Why it's here: Accessible entry point into the formal, information-theoretic backing for "best frame = maximum task-relevant fidelity per unit of representational cost" — the same minimum-description-length intuition stated as a control-theory theorem rather than a cognitive-science claim.
- Accessibility: [CLAUDE] [MARKDOWN]
- **Flag for verification:** Wikipedia is a tertiary source on a contested-in-its-precise-form theorem (see the LessWrong "Fixing the Good Regulator Theorem" critique surfaced during research) — useful as an accessible overview, but the original Conant & Ashby paper or a peer-reviewed restatement should be consulted for anything load-bearing.
